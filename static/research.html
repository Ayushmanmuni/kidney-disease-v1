🤖 Model Training Methodology

The training pipeline follows a rigorous process to ensure maximum accuracy and generalizability:

1. Data Cleaning & Imputation

The raw dataset contains approximately 20% missing values. A combined imputation strategy is used: KNN Imputer for numerical features (considering the 5 nearest neighbors) and mode imputation for categorical features. Records with more than 50% missing values are excluded.

2. Feature Encoding

Categorical features are label-encoded into binary (0/1) or ordinal values. Features like Red Blood Cells ("normal"→1, "abnormal"→0), Pus Cell Clumps ("present"→1, "notpresent"→0), and Hypertension ("yes"→1, "no"→0) are converted to numerical form.

3. Dataset Splitting

The cleaned dataset is split 80/20 into training and test sets using stratified sampling to maintain the CKD/not-CKD ratio in both partitions.

4. Multi-Model Comparison

Five different algorithms are trained and compared:

Model Accuracy Key Strengths
Random Forest~99%Best overall accuracy, robust to outliers, handles mixed feature types
Gradient Boosting~98%Strong sequential learning, excellent on tabular data
SVM (RBF Kernel)~97%Effective in high-dimensional spaces, good margin separation
Logistic Regression~96%Fast, interpretable, good baseline for binary classification
K-Nearest Neighbors~95%Simple, instance-based, effective with normalized features

5. Cross-Validation

5-fold stratified cross-validation is used to assess model stability. The Random Forest model achieves consistent accuracy across all folds, confirming it is not overfitting to any specific data partition.

6. Model Selection & Serialization

The Random Forest Classifier is selected as the production model based on the highest cross-validated accuracy. It is serialized to disk using joblib as best_ckd_model.pkl and loaded by the Flask server on startup.

📈 Top Predictive Features

Features ranked by their importance in the Random Forest model

1

Hemoglobin (hemo)

The most important predictor. Low hemoglobin levels strongly correlate with CKD due to reduced erythropoietin production by damaged kidneys.

2

Specific Gravity (sg)

Measures urine concentration. Damaged kidneys lose the ability to concentrate urine, resulting in lower specific gravity values.

3

Albumin (al)

Protein in urine (albuminuria) is an early marker of kidney damage. Higher albumin levels in urine indicate glomerular dysfunction.

4

Packed Cell Volume (pcv)

Percentage of blood volume occupied by red blood cells. Lower PCV reflects anemia, a common CKD complication.

5

Serum Creatinine (sc)

A key kidney function marker. High serum creatinine indicates the kidneys are not filtering waste effectively.

⚖️ Limitations & Ethical Considerations

  • Dataset size: 400 records is relatively small. Performance may vary on broader, more diverse populations.
  • Geographic bias: Data was collected from a single hospital in India and may not generalize to all demographics.
  • Missing data: ~20% of original values were imputed, which introduces some uncertainty.
  • Not a clinical tool: This system is for educational and screening purposes only. It is NOT a substitute for professional medical diagnosis.
  • Binary classification: The model predicts CKD/Not-CKD but does not stage the disease (Stage 1–5).
  • Temporal limitation: The model does not account for changes over time — it analyzes a single snapshot of biomarkers.
