Understanding the Disease

Chronic Kidney Disease

A comprehensive guide to understanding CKD — its causes, symptoms, global impact, and why early detection can save lives.

🫘 What is Chronic Kidney Disease?

Chronic Kidney Disease (CKD) is the gradual loss of kidney function over months or years. Your kidneys are two bean-shaped organs, each about the size of a fist, located on either side of the spine. They perform several critical functions:

  • Filtering blood — removing waste products and excess fluids from the body
  • Balancing electrolytes — maintaining proper levels of sodium, potassium, and calcium
  • Regulating blood pressure — producing hormones that help control blood pressure
  • Red blood cell production — producing erythropoietin (EPO) that stimulates bone marrow
  • Vitamin D activation — converting vitamin D to its active form for bone health

When CKD progresses, damaged kidneys can no longer filter waste effectively. Waste products build up to dangerous levels in the blood, leading to serious complications including cardiovascular disease, anemia, bone disease, and eventually kidney failure.

⚠️ Causes & Risk Factors

CKD can be caused by a variety of conditions. The two most common causes account for nearly two-thirds of all cases:

🍬

Diabetes (Type 1 & 2)

The #1 cause of CKD. High blood sugar damages the millions of tiny blood vessels (glomeruli) in the kidneys that filter waste. About 1 in 3 diabetic adults develops CKD.

💉

High Blood Pressure

The #2 cause of CKD. Uncontrolled hypertension damages the blood vessels in and around the kidneys, reducing their ability to function. About 1 in 5 adults with high BP develops CKD.

🧬

Other Risk Factors

Glomerulonephritis, polycystic kidney disease, prolonged obstruction of the urinary tract, recurrent kidney infections, obesity, smoking, age over 60, and family history of kidney disease.

🔍 Signs & Symptoms

CKD is often called a "silent disease" because in early stages there may be no symptoms at all. As kidney function declines, symptoms gradually appear:

😴
Fatigue & Weakness

Reduced kidney function leads to a buildup of toxins and impurities in the blood, causing tiredness.

🦶
Swelling (Edema)

Puffy eyes, swollen ankles and feet due to fluid retention when kidneys can't remove excess fluid.

🚽
Changes in Urination

More frequent urination (especially at night), foamy or bubbly urine, or blood in urine.

🤢
Nausea & Loss of Appetite

Buildup of uremic toxins causes nausea, vomiting, and a general loss of interest in food.

🥶
Feeling Cold

Anemia caused by CKD can make you feel cold all the time, even in warm environments.

🧠
Difficulty Concentrating

Toxins in the brain from kidney disease cause confusion, difficulty thinking, and memory problems.

Disease Progression

The 5 Stages of CKD

CKD progresses through stages based on glomerular filtration rate (GFR) — how well your kidneys filter blood

1

Stage 1 — Mild Damage

GFR ≥ 90 mL/min

Kidney damage is present but with normal or high kidney function. Usually no symptoms. Often discovered through other tests. Kidneys are still working well but there is evidence of damage (e.g., protein in urine).

Action: Monitor, control blood sugar and blood pressure.

2

Stage 2 — Early Decline

GFR 60–89 mL/min

Mild loss of function. Kidneys still work well enough that most people have no symptoms. The damage found in Stage 1 is confirmed with a mild decrease in GFR.

Action: Regular checkups, healthy lifestyle, manage risk factors.

3

Stage 3 — Moderate Damage

GFR 30–59 mL/min

Noticeable loss of function. This is often where symptoms first appear: fatigue, fluid retention, back pain, and changes in urination. Split into 3A (45–59) and 3B (30–44). Waste products may begin building up in the blood.

Action: See nephrologist, dietary changes, manage complications.

4

Stage 4 — Severe Damage

GFR 15–29 mL/min

Severe reduction in kidney function. At this stage, complications like anemia, high blood pressure, and bone disease are common. Patients need to prepare for dialysis or transplant.

Action: Plan for dialysis or kidney transplant. Specialist care required.

5

Stage 5 — Kidney Failure (ESRD)

GFR < 15 mL/min

End-Stage Renal Disease. Kidneys have lost almost all their ability to function. Without treatment (dialysis or transplant), this is a life-threatening condition. Toxins, fluids, and electrolytes build up to dangerous levels.

Action: Dialysis or kidney transplant is essential for survival.

⏰ Why Early Detection Matters

Early-stage CKD (Stages 1–2) is treatable and manageable. With proper lifestyle changes, medication, and monitoring, the progression to kidney failure can often be significantly delayed or even prevented.
